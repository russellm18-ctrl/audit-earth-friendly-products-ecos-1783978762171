## Atmospheric Reality Gap

Your lifestyle shot — the kitchen scene with the family and dog — does exactly what it needs to do emotionally: warm ambient light, genuine movement, product visible but not forced. The ECOS spray bottle sits on the island in the left third of the frame, readable, correctly color-matched to your brand palette. That image earns its placement.

The product shots are where the gap opens. The Magnolia & Lily laundry detergent photograph is well-executed — controlled studio light, lily props echo the scent story, label legibility is clean. The Grapefruit dish soap shot directly contradicts it. The backdrop is a wrinkled fabric in a saturated, uneven yellow-green — a surface that reads as low-budget prop styling against the more polished lily setup. When a buyer moves between these two frames, they are not seeing one brand. They are seeing two different production budgets, which signals inconsistency in the underlying operation. Your copy claims "the most authentic, sustainable and affordable eco-conscious cleaning products for all." Inconsistent production quality makes "authentic" harder to believe without additional evidence.

The trust cost: a retail buyer or wholesale partner evaluating your brand visually before a call has already discounted your premium positioning before the conversation starts.

## Visual Narrative Coherence

Your site carries 41 images on the homepage. The scan confirms 0 broken links across 25 checked URLs — the infrastructure is sound. The fracture is not technical; it is tonal. The category carousel (Images 12 through 31 in the scan) loops the same five category thumbnails — Laundry, Dishwashing, Household Cleaners, Hand Soap, Pets — repeating the identical image assets across at least five carousel rotations. That repetition is visible in the source: `ecos_np_aw_0187_Ret_04182023-scaled-updated.jpeg` and `ECOS-dish-soap-1.jpeg` each appear in the DOM four to five times.

A high-value retail or licensing buyer scrolling the homepage does not see a rich catalog. They see one product photograph cycled on a loop. It signals a shallow visual library at precisely the moment you need them to believe you have the depth and range to occupy shelf space across multiple categories. The grapefruit and lily shots you have attached prove you are investing in product photography — that investment is invisible at the homepage level because the carousel never surfaces them.

## Metadata and Conversion Gaps

Your page title runs 69 characters — within acceptable range — and your meta description hits 160 characters, which is the functional ceiling. The og:image is present. On those three counts, you are not losing search real estate. You rank #1 for your brand name, so branded search is captured.

The problem is the 1 image out of 41 missing alt text (2% gap per the scan). That specific image is the full-bleed hero loaded from `ECOS_FORDER_293_EDIT-scaled.jpg` — the largest visual asset on the page and the first content Google Images indexes. A missing alt on your highest-weight image is not a minor accessibility checkbox. It is the one frame where a descriptive, keyword-carrying alt string — "plant-powered laundry detergent family kitchen ECOS" — would do measurable work. Screen readers skip it entirely, and Google Images cannot categorize it, which forfeits organic image-search impressions on your top-of-funnel asset.

Supporting this: your product image filenames — `ECOS-dish-soap-1.jpeg`, `ecos_np_aw_0187_Ret_04182023-scaled-updated.jpeg` — carry no scent variant, no SKU context, no ingredient signal. Every product search that includes a modifier ("grapefruit dish soap plant-based") bypasses your imagery entirely because the filenames give crawlers nothing to match against.

You are ranked #1 for your name. You are not ranked for your products. That is the conversion gap.
